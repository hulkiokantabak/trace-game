## THE MUSICIAN'S FRAGMENTS (Tone: Musical, rhythmic, rests in the prose)

**M-F1 (First anomaly)** — Found at: L01 (Canal Basin)
Under the Limehouse Basin the water carries a note. B-flat, sustained, source unknown. The boat painters hear it in winter when the engines stop. They don't discuss it. They hum along without noticing.

**M-F2 (Historical precedent)** — Found at: L05 (The Grapes)
In 1967 a recording engineer set up equipment in a Narrow Street basement. The session tapes contain voices he didn't record — a woman singing in a dialect forty years out of date. He labelled the tape "background" and never listened again. The tape is in the pub's walls now.

**M-F3 (Mechanism suggested)** — Found at: G07 (Bookshop)
Limestone absorbs vibration. The clay beneath London is forty metres deep in places. A geoacoustics paper from 1983 proposed that certain geological formations could store and replay sound energy. The paper was retracted. The author moved to Limehouse.

**M-F4 (Complication)** — Found at: L04 (Warehouse Studio)
The Sound Artist's spectrogram of Limehouse at 3 AM shows frequencies below human hearing — and above it. They form patterns. Not random. Not musical exactly. Conversational. As if two sources were responding to each other across a frequency gap no human voice could bridge.

**M-F5 (Personal role implied)** — Found at: L03 (St Anne's)
Hawksmoor's churches were built to specific acoustic specifications. St Anne's resonates at B-flat. The same note as the canal. The same note the barista hums. The question nobody asks: does the church amplify the city's sound, or does the city tune itself to the church?

**M-F6 (Watcher's trace)** — Found at: L08 (DLR Platform)
Someone left a notebook in the Limehouse DLR station in 2019. Forty pages of frequency logs, dated and precise. The last entry: "The signal is no longer in the background. It's in the foreground. I've been hearing it for three years. I think it's been hearing me for longer." No name.

**M-F7 (The question)** — Found at: L10 (Empty Lot)
There's a frequency at which memory becomes sound and sound becomes memory. Every city has one. London's is the oldest. It has been accumulating for two thousand years. The question is not whether you can hear it. The question is what happens when it hears you listening.

## THE PHOTOGRAPHER'S FRAGMENTS (Tone: Precise, documentary, clinical until the last sentence goes wrong)

**P-F1 (First anomaly)** — Found at: G06 (Naval College)
The Painted Hall ceiling contains 40,000 square feet of painted surface. A 2015 restoration documented every inch. In the north-east corner, behind Neptune's trident, they found a brushstroke in a pigment not manufactured until 1923. The restoration report lists it as "contamination." Nobody contaminated it.

**P-F2 (Historical precedent)** — Found at: BI-05 (Bermondsey Street)
The London Survey of 1746 recorded every building on Bermondsey Street. Three buildings in the survey do not appear on any subsequent map. They were not demolished — no demolition record exists. They are simply absent from every document after 1746. The addresses still receive mail.

**P-F3 (Mechanism suggested)** — Found at: G02 (Observatory)
A daguerreotype from 1842 of the Greenwich waterfront shows, in the reflection on the Thames, a building that wasn't on the north bank until 1856. Fourteen years early. The reflection is sharper than the subject — as if the river's version was more real than the photographer's.

**P-F4 (Complication)** — Found at: B02 (Gallery)
The gallery's storage contains a canvas with no acquisition record. The painting shows Bermondsey as it will look in 2035 — at least, according to the planning applications on file. The paint is old. The linen is Victorian. The scene is nine years from now. The Gallery Owner has hung it facing the wall.

**P-F5 (Personal role implied)** — Found at: BI-06 (Rooftop)
From the rooftop of the railway arch, the streets of Bermondsey form letters. Not in any alphabet currently in use. The letters are legible only from above and only at twilight when shadows extend the buildings' edges. Someone designed this. Not the urban planners. The city arranged itself into a sentence nobody has read.

**P-F6 (Watcher's trace)** — Found at: L06 (Tattoo Parlour)
A sketchbook found in the Tattoo Parlour's back room. Not the artist's. Dated 2018-2021. Three years of drawings: the same street corner in Bermondsey, drawn daily, from the same angle. Each drawing is identical except for one building that moves. By the final drawing it has rotated forty-five degrees. The sketchbook's owner is unnamed.

**P-F7 (The question)** — Found at: G09 (Thames Foreshore)
The Thames is London's mirror. Every edition of the city has looked at its reflection in the river and seen itself. But a mirror doesn't just show what's there. A mirror reverses. What if the Thames shows London not as it is, but as its negative — the version that exists on the other side of the reflection? And what if that version is more complete?

## THE WANDERER'S FRAGMENTS (Tone: Drifting, impressionistic, syntax that wanders before arriving)

**W-F1 (First anomaly)** — Found at: L10 (Empty Lot)
There's a place in Limehouse where the wind stops. Not sheltered — there are no walls. The air simply decides to be still. Dogs avoid it. Children play near it instinctively. It feels the way a room feels when someone has just left — warm, shaped by an absence.

**W-F2 (Historical precedent)** — Found at: G04 (Foot Tunnel)
In 1902 when the Greenwich tunnel was dug, workers reported a reluctance — not their own — at a specific depth. The clay resisted. Not physically. Emotionally. Three workers quit on the same day. They described, independently, a feeling of being watched by something that was not above them.

**W-F3 (Mechanism suggested)** — Found at: B07 (Old Church)
Every building accumulates the emotions of its inhabitants. Hospitals feel different from schools. Prisons feel different from libraries. This isn't metaphor. Studies in environmental psychology measure it. But nobody has measured a city. Nobody has asked what two thousand years of accumulated feeling produces. Perhaps because the answer moves.

**W-F4 (Complication)** — Found at: G08 (Park Path)
Greenwich Park at dawn, before the joggers and the dog walkers, holds a feeling that doesn't belong to the present. It's older. Not sad. Not happy. Anticipatory. As if the park is waiting for someone it expected centuries ago. The feeling dissipates by 7 AM. The park pretends it was never there.

**W-F5 (Personal role implied)** — Found at: L01 (Canal Basin)
Some people arrive in a city and the city notices. Not everyone. Not most. But some walk down a street and the street's temperature changes — not in the air, in the feeling. The city extends something toward them. An attention. A curiosity. If you've felt it — the sense of a place leaning toward you — you already know. If you haven't, this fragment means nothing.

**W-F6 (Watcher's trace)** — Found at: B05 (Thames Path)
A message scratched into the Thames Path railing near Rotherhithe, so faint you'd miss it unless you were looking. Or unless you were feeling. It reads: "It noticed me in 2017. I noticed it back. That was my mistake. The noticing doesn't stop. Walk away if you can."

**W-F7 (The question)** — Found at: B04 (Warehouse)
London has dreamed every night for two thousand years. Dreams process what the waking mind cannot. If London were to wake — if the dream ended — where would the unprocessed go? The sadness of every goodbye at every train station. The joy of every first kiss on every bridge. The weight of it. The sheer weight. Would London survive its own waking?

## THE BARISTA'S FRAGMENTS (Tone: Conversational, overheard, pub-gossip energy)

**BA-F1 (First anomaly)** — Found at: L02 (Coffee Shop)
The regulars at the Limehouse coffee shop all arrived in the same month. Different reasons, different backgrounds, different stories. But they all moved to Limehouse in October 2023. The barista noticed because she writes the names on the cups. Nobody else noticed. Nobody thinks it's strange. It's very strange.

**BA-F2 (Historical precedent)** — Found at: G09 (Foreshore)
In 1888, a vicar in Bermondsey compiled a list of every person who had lived on his street for the previous century. The list showed that no two households were unconnected — every family had a link to at least one other, through marriage, employment, or a shared event. The vicar called it "the web." He stopped compiling when the web began to include people who hadn't been born yet.

**BA-F3 (Mechanism suggested)** — Found at: B06 (Co-Working Space)
Network theory says six degrees separate any two people on earth. In London the number is three. In East London it's two. The connections aren't social media friendships. They're spatial — sharing a bus, breathing the same air, walking the same pavement within minutes of each other. The city is a contact network. Everyone touches everyone.

**BA-F4 (Complication)** — Found at: L09 (Night Market)
The Nightclub Promoter keeps a list. Not a guest list — a connection list. Who knows who. Who met who and where. The list has a gap. A node where connections converge but nobody's there. Like a roundabout with all roads leading to it and an empty center. She's been maintaining the list for four years. The gap has been there since she started.

**BA-F5 (Personal role implied)** — Found at: G10 (Pub)
You know the way a bartender knows everyone? Knows their drink, their story, their Tuesday mood versus their Friday mood? Imagine a city that knows its people that way. Every street a bartender. Every corner a regular. The city takes your order before you know what you want. It seats you next to the person you need to meet. And if someone is removed from the bar, everyone feels the missing stool.

**BA-F6 (Watcher's trace)** — Found at: G03 (St Alfege)
A page torn from a journal, found pressed inside a hymnal at St Alfege. The handwriting is controlled but the content is not: "I erased them because the alternative was worse. A single node carrying the weight of every connection in London. A person becoming the city. I broke the web to save it. I broke the web. I broke it."

**BA-F7 (The question)** — Found at: BI-09 (Child's drawing location)
The erased name is not gone. It's distributed. Split across every person who ever knew them, like a word broken into letters scattered through a book. The letters are still there. The word can be reassembled. But should it be? A name that powerful, a connection that total — is that a person, or is that something a person should never be?

## THE SHOPKEEPER'S FRAGMENTS (Tone: Stubborn, inscriptional, facts that don't require belief)

**S-F1 (First anomaly)** — Found at: B01 (Antiques Market)
The key has no manufacturer's mark from any known foundry. The alloy contains traces of a mineral found only in the chalk deposits beneath the Thames Basin. The mineral has no industrial application. It has been in the ground since before the ice retreated. The key was made from the ground itself.

**S-F2 (Historical precedent)** — Found at: G01 (Clockmaker's Shop)
Seven stones in London have never moved. Not since they were placed. The London Stone on Cannon Street is the famous one. Six others are unmarked, embedded in walls and foundations across the city. Each predates the building it supports. Each weighs more than it should. The clockmaker's shop is built on one.

**S-F3 (Mechanism suggested)** — Found at: B07 (Old Church)
Geologists classify the ground beneath London as inert. Clay, gravel, chalk. But inert means "not observed to move." A substance can be inert for ten thousand years and active for ten seconds and the geologists would still call it inert. The geological record measures centuries. The thing beneath London measures millennia. Its last movement was before anyone was keeping records.

**S-F4 (Complication)** — Found at: L07 (Lock Gates)
The lock gates at Limehouse Basin are anchored to a foundation poured in 1820. The foundation has not cracked, settled, or shifted in two centuries. This is unusual. London clay shifts. Everything built on it shifts. The lock gates should have moved. They haven't. They're held by something beneath the foundation that the builders never found because they stopped digging when the ground got warm.

**S-F5 (Personal role implied)** — Found at: B03 (Railway Arch)
Some people feel the ground more than others. Not seismologists — not people with instruments. People with hands. Bricklayers who know which wall will stand and which won't. Gardeners who feel the soil's mood. Shopkeepers who've stood on the same floor for thirty years and can tell you when the building sighs. These people don't know what they know. But the ground knows them.

**S-F6 (Watcher's trace)** — Found at: B04 (Warehouse)
A survey marker embedded in the warehouse floor. Official-looking but not from any known survey agency. Dated 2020. The coordinates are correct. The depth reading is not — it shows a measurement that extends 400 metres below ground level. Standard surveys reach 30. Next to the marker, scratched in the concrete: "Still sleeping. Check annually."

**S-F7 (The question)** — Found at: L03 (St Anne's)
London was not built on this ground. London was grown from it. The streets are veins. The buildings are bones. The parks are lungs. The Thames is the breath. And beneath all of it, the thing that grew the city sleeps. If it wakes, it won't destroy London. It will become London. The city and the foundation will be the same thing. The question is whether that has already happened, and nobody noticed, and London has been alive the whole time.
